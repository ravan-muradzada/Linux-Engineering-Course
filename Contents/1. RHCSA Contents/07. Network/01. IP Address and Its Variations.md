#network #theory 

## **What is IP?**

IP stands for Internet Protocol. It's a set of rules that governs how data is addressed and routed across networks. Every device on a network gets an IP address — think of it as a postal address for your machine. Without it, devices wouldn't know where to send or receive data. 

----

## **What is Adapter?**

An adapter (also called a network interface) is the hardware or virtual component in your machine that connects it to a network. In terms of IP, it is the thing that actually gets assigned an IP address. Your machine can have multiple adapters — for example one for ethernet, one for Wi-Fi — and each one can have its own IP address.

In RHEL you'll see them named things like `eth0`, `ens33`, or `enp0s3`. The loopback interface `lo` is also technically an adapter, just a virtual one with no physical hardware behind it.

Short answer: adapter = the interface that holds the IP address on your machine.

---
## **What is Loopback IP?**

The loopback address is `127.0.0.1`, and its hostname is `localhost`. It's a virtual network interface that your system uses to communicate with itself. When you ping `127.0.0.1`, the packet never actually leaves your machine — it loops back internally. It's heavily used for testing services locally. The entire `127.0.0.0/8` range is reserved for loopback, but `127.0.0.1` is the standard one you'll always see.

----

## **What is Private and Public IP?**

A public IP is globally routable — it's what identifies your network on the internet, assigned by your ISP. A private IP is used inside local networks (your home, office, lab) and is not routable on the public internet. Routers use NAT (Network Address Translation) to bridge between private and public. Private ranges are standardized by RFC 1918, which brings us to classes.

----

## **Private IP Classes - A, B, C**

##### **Class A**
This class covers `10.0.0.0` to `10.255.255.255` (or `10.0.0.0/8`). It supports a massive number of hosts — over 16 million — so it's used by large enterprises and cloud providers.

##### **Class B**
This class covers `172.16.0.0` to `172.31.255.255` (or `172.16.0.0/12`). It's a medium range, supporting around 1 million hosts, commonly used in mid-sized corporate networks.

##### **Class C**
This class covers `192.168.0.0` to `192.168.255.255` (or `192.168.0.0/16`). This is what you'll see most in home networks and small labs — including your RHEL practice environment almost certainly. It supports about 65,000 host addresses across all its subnets.

## **Loopback IP vs Private IP**

Both loopback and private IPs are unreachable from the public internet, so on the surface they seem similar. But they are fundamentally different things.

A private IP (like `192.168.1.10`) is assigned to a real network interface — your ethernet card, Wi-Fi adapter, etc. Other devices on your local network can reach you through it. It travels through actual network hardware.

Loopback (`127.0.0.1`) never touches any network interface at all — not even your local one. It exists only inside the kernel of your own machine. No other device on your network can reach you via `127.0.0.1`, not even your router sitting one meter away. It is exclusively self-communication.

So the key difference is: private IP = visible to your local network. Loopback = visible only to yourself, on your own machine, at the OS level.

A useful way to think about it — private IP is like your home address that your neighbors can use to find you. Loopback is like talking to yourself in your own head. Nobody else is involved at all.

---
## **Is Public IP Device-Specific?**

**No** — in almost all cases, a public IP is not assigned to your individual device.

Here is what actually happens. Your ISP assigns one public IP to your router/modem. Every device in your home or office — your laptop, phone, RHEL server — has a private IP internally. When any of those devices sends traffic to the internet, your router replaces the private source IP with its own public IP before sending it out. This is NAT, which works here. From the internet's perspective, all your devices look like one single public IP.

----
## **What is Subnet?**

A subnet (short for ***subnetwork***) is a smaller, logical division of a larger network. Instead of having all devices in one giant network, you split it into smaller groups. This improves organization, security, and performance. For example in a company, the HR department and the engineering department can be on separate subnets — they're on the same physical building network but logically separated.
