#crontab #commands 

`at` is a command that schedules a job to run **once** at a specific time, then it's done. [[01. Introduction to Crontab|Crontab]] is for **repeating** jobs.

```bash
at 15:30
at> cp /etc/passwd /tmp/passwd_backup
at>
```

`at` is much more flexible than just hour:minute. You can write very human-friendly time expressions:

```bash
at 15:30 tomorrow
at 2:00 PM next Friday
at now + 2 hours
at now + 3 days
at midnight
at noon
at 23:59 December 31
```

So it supports specific dates, relative times, and even plain English words like `midnight` and `noon`. Much more readable than cron syntax, but again — runs **once** and it's done.

`at` doesn't accept cron-style syntax like `30 14 * * 5`. It has its own time format as you just saw.

If you need cron-style scheduling, use crontab. If you need a one-time job with flexible human-readable time, use `at`. They're designed for different purposes.
