## **TCP (Transmission Control Protocol)**

TCP is a connection-oriented protocol. Before any data is sent, it establishes a connection between two sides through a process called the **three-way handshake**. After that, every packet sent is acknowledged by the receiver. If a packet is lost, TCP detects it and resends it. This guarantees that all data arrives completely, in order, and without corruption.

The cost of this reliability is speed — all that acknowledging and error checking adds overhead.

TCP is used where accuracy matters more than speed — web browsing (HTTP/HTTPS), SSH, email, file transfers.

---

## **UDP (User Datagram Protocol)**
 
UDP is connectionless. It just fires packets at the destination without establishing a connection first, without acknowledging receipt, and without caring about lost packets. No handshake, no guarantees, no retransmission.

The benefit is speed — no overhead means much faster communication.

UDP is used where speed matters more than perfection — video streaming, online gaming, voice calls, DNS. A slightly corrupted video frame is acceptable, but lag in a live call is not.

---

## **The core difference**

TCP is like sending a registered mail — you get confirmation every step of the way. UDP is like throwing flyers out of a window — fast, but you have no idea who actually caught one.

Usage TCP or UDP in one application is determined only by its creator.
