## **What is NTP?**

NTP stands for **Network Time Protocol**. It's how computers sync their clocks over a [[09. Usage of nmtui|network]].

Why does it matter? Because in Linux (and especially in DevOps), a lot of things depend on accurate time — [[01. Introduction to Logging|logs]], [[02. Crontab Command|scheduled jobs]], SSL certificates, Kerberos authentication, and distributed systems all break or behave weirdly if clocks are off.

**How it works simply:** Your system connects to a time server (like `pool.ntp.org`), compares its clock, and adjusts. It does this continuously to stay accurate.

On [[02. Introduction to Red Hat|RHEL]], the tool used is **Chrony** (not the old `ntpd`). The key commands you'll use:

- `chronyc tracking` — shows your sync status
- `timedatectl` — shows/sets time, timezone, and NTP status
- `timedatectl set-ntp true` — enables NTP sync

For RHCSA, Chrony configuration (`/etc/chrony.conf`) and being able to verify sync status are the practical things to know.
