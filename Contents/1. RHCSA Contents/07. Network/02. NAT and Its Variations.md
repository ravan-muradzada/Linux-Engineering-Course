#network #theory 

## **What is NAT?**

**NAT** (Network Address Translation) is the process that our router performs to translate [[01. IP Address and Its Variations|private ip address to public ip address]] and vice versa. So, our devices have private IPs, our router has one public IP, and NAT is mechanism that makes a communication between them. Without NAT, your private IPs would be invisible to the internet, which is exactly the point.

There are three kinds of **NAT**: Static NAT, Dynamic NAT, PAT.

----

## **What is Static NAT?**

**Static NAT** is a one-to-one mapping — one private IP permanently maps to one public IP. Every time that specific device communicates, it always uses that same public IP. *This is used for servers that need to be consistently reachable from the internet, like a web server.*

---
## **What is Dynamic NAT?**

**Dynamic NAT** is also one-to-one, but not permanent. You have a pool of public IPs and they get assigned to private IPs on demand. When a device needs internet access it grabs an available public IP from the pool, and releases it when done. Less common today.

---

## **What is PAT (Port Address Translation)?**

**PAT (Port Address Translation)** — also called NAT Overload — is what virtually every home and office router uses. This is many-to-one. Multiple private IPs all share a single public IP, but each connection is tracked using different port numbers. Your router keeps a table of which internal device owns which port, so responses come back to the right machine.
