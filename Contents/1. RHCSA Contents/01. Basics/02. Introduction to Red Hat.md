## **What is Red Hat?**

Red Hat is a company that takes the Linux kernel and builds an enterprise-level distribution called Red Hat Enterprise Linux (RHEL). The difference between Red Hat and many other distributions (like Ubuntu, Debian, Arch) is focus. Red Hat focuses on stability, long-term support, security updates, enterprise environments, and corporate infrastructure. Companies use RHEL for servers, data centers, banks, telecom systems. It is not about “latest features”; it is about reliability and support contracts.

Red Hat is also important because it built a business model around open source. The software is open, but companies pay for support, updates, certification, and official maintenance. That model made Linux trusted in enterprise environments.

## **What is CentOs?**

**CentOS** (Community ENTerprise Operating System) was a free, community-supported Linux distribution built from the source code of Red Hat Enterprise Linux (RHEL).

Here is the key idea:

Red Hat releases RHEL as open-source software (because Linux is GPL licensed). Anyone can legally take the RHEL source code, remove Red Hat trademarks, rebuild it, and distribute it. That is exactly what CentOS originally did. It was basically **RHEL without the Red Hat branding and paid support**.

So historically:

- RHEL → Paid, enterprise support, official updates
- CentOS → Free rebuild of RHEL, binary-compatible

Binary-compatible means software that works on RHEL also works on CentOS. That is why many companies used CentOS on servers to save money while keeping RHEL-level stability.

Relationship timeline:

- Initially, CentOS was fully community-driven.
- In 2014, Red Hat officially took control of the CentOS project.
- In 2020, Red Hat changed the model and shifted focus to **CentOS Stream**.

CentOS Stream is different. It is not a direct rebuild of RHEL. Instead, it sits **between Fedora and RHEL** in the development pipeline:

Fedora → CentOS Stream → RHEL

That means CentOS Stream receives updates _before_ they go into RHEL. So it is slightly ahead, less stable than classic CentOS, but still enterprise-oriented.

Because of this change, the original CentOS Linux (stable RHEL clone) was discontinued. This decision caused many companies to move to alternatives like:

- AlmaLinux
- Rocky Linux

Both aim to be 1:1 RHEL-compatible like old CentOS.

For RHCSA understanding:

- RHEL = enterprise, paid, stable
- Old CentOS = free rebuild of RHEL
- CentOS Stream = development preview of next RHEL

Today, if your goal is RHCSA, focus on RHEL behavior, not old CentOS.
