## **What is MAC address?**

MAC (Media Access Control) address is a unique identifier assigned to a network adapter by its manufacturer. It's a hardware-level address, burned into the physical adapter itself. It looks like this: `00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E` — 6 pairs of hexadecimal numbers, 48 bits total.

While IP address is a logical address (can change, can be assigned, can be private/public), MAC address is a physical address tied to the hardware itself. In theory it's permanent, though in practice it can be spoofed in software.

---

## **How it relates to what we covered**

IP works at the network level — it handles routing between different networks, getting your data from your home to a server across the internet. But inside your local network (same subnet), devices actually communicate using MAC addresses, not IPs. This is where **ARP (Address Resolution Protocol)** comes in — when your machine knows the IP of another device on the same network but needs its MAC address to actually deliver the data, it broadcasts an ARP request saying "who has this IP, tell me your MAC." The target device responds with its MAC, and communication happens.

So the relationship is — IP gets the data to the right network, MAC gets it to the right device within that network. They work at different layers and complement each other.

---

## **From Internet to Your Device — How Data Actually Travels**

When you make a request to the internet, your device sends it to the router. The router takes your private IP, translates it to its public IP via PAT, and sends it out. The internet only ever sees the public IP — your private IP and MAC never leave your local network.

When the response comes back, it arrives at the router's public IP. The router checks its PAT table to find which private IP made that request and forwards the data internally. But at this point, knowing the private IP is still not enough to physically deliver the data to your device — IP handles routing between networks, not physical delivery within a local network. For the final delivery inside the local network, MAC address is needed. The router checks its ARP table, finds the MAC address that corresponds to your private IP, and delivers the data directly to your device.

This is why both exist and neither replaces the other. Public IP gets data across the internet to your router. Private IP gets it to the right internal destination. MAC delivers it physically to the right device. They operate at different levels and hand off to each other in exactly this sequence.
