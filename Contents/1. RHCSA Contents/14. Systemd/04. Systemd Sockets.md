#systemd #sockets


## **What is Socket?**

A socket is a **communication endpoint** — a way for two processes to talk to each other.

Think of it like a door with a mailbox. One process listens at that door, another knocks and passes data through it.

In systemd there's a `socket` [[02. Units|unit]] type (`.socket` files). The idea is simple — instead of starting a service immediately at boot, systemd **opens the socket and waits**. When something actually connects to that socket, _then_ systemd starts the real service.

This is called **socket activation**.

---

## **Why is this useful?**

It saves resources. The service isn't running until someone actually needs it. Also speeds up boot time because you're not starting everything upfront.

We mainly just need to know that when we see a `.socket` file paired with a `.service` file, they work together — the socket watches for connections, the service does the actual work.

Example socket file as example:

```bash
cat /usr/lib/systemd/system/sshd.socket
```
